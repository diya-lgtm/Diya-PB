Post 01 — Cookie deprecation reality check

We were on a call with a retail CMO last week.

She told us her team had built their entire 2026 personalization plan around third-party cookies that no longer work in 40% of the web.

She did not know it.

This is happening at almost every retailer we talk to.

The deprecation timeline has been pushed back so many times that everyone stopped paying attention.

Then it actually started happening.

The behavioral data you used to rent from third parties is gone.

The first party data you own is not enough on its own.

What works now is real-time prediction from the signals you can still see on your own site.

Clicks. Scroll patterns. Time on page.

This is exactly what we built Quin to do.

Not because we predicted this perfectly, because Gonca and I never trusted borrowed data in the first place.

Post 02 — Building with my sister

I get this question almost every week.

"What is it like building a company with your sister?"

When Gonca and I started Quin, the warning we got most often was that family co-founder relationships are the riskiest of all.

Disagreements get personal. Holidays get awkward.

Five years in, here is the truth.

Building with someone who has known me since I was three years old is the single biggest advantage I have.

There is no political layer.

There is no managing up. We get from disagreement to decision in 20 minutes because we do not need to perform for each other.

The cost is real too.

My mother has banned both of us from talking about Quin at her house. Twice.

If you are thinking about building with a family member, the only test that matters: can you tell them they are wrong, in front of other people, without it breaking the relationship?

Post 03 — What M&S and IKEA actually want from AI

People assume what enterprise brands want from AI is the future of magical personalization.

The Black Mirror version where the AI predicts what you want before you do.

That is not what they want. At all.

What M&S and IKEA actually ask for, every meeting, every quarter: show me which 5% of visitors are about to bounce.

Show me which 1% is buying right now if we just stay out of their way.

The boring version of AI is the version that wins in enterprise retail.

The flashy version wins at pitch competitions.

Three years of building Quin taught me this the hard way.

We burned runway on features no one used.

The version of AI that quietly increases conversion by 3% across 10 million sessions is worth more than the version that makes 10 people on Twitter say wow.
