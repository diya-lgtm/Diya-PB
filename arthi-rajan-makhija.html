Post 01 — The operator perspective no one else has

I managed a $1B+ loss budget. Ran teams across four countries. Led the integration of two acquisitions, each with its own P&L, roadmap, and revenue targets.

That is not a fraud background. That is a business background that happens to be expressed through fraud.

Most people in risk see the problem through one lens. Either as a technologist who wants to build better models, or as an operator who wants to write tighter policies. I have had to hold both at the same time, inside a Fortune 500 company, with real revenue on the line.

What that taught me is this. Fraud is never just a fraud problem. It is always a business problem wearing a fraud mask. When you treat it as anything less, you end up with metrics that look good on paper and customers who are quietly leaving.

That is the thing we built Pinch to fix.

Post 02 — The founder origin story

I am not a very active poster on LinkedIn. That is not a humblebrag. It is just true.

For two decades I was heads down at the intersection of fraud, identity, and payments. Not as an observer. As an operator. The work was loud. My voice was not.

After I left, I took a CPO role at a healthcare startup. Building outside a large enterprise taught me things I could not have learned any other way. How to move fast with incomplete information. How to make calls when no one above you is signing off. How to stay resilient when the org chart stops being a safety net.

That experience made me a better founder than I would have been if I had come straight from a large company.

Today I run Pinch AI. We help ecommerce brands stop return abuse and first-party fraud, turning returns from a cost center into a strategic control point. I started writing here because the things we are seeing inside enterprise retail right now are not showing up in the reports. I want to change that.

Post 03 — What it costs when you get it wrong

A reporter asked me last week why there are so few women in fraud and risk.

I gave the polite answer. Then I sat with the real one for a few days.

Fraud is a domain that rewards two things at the same time: deep technical pattern recognition and deep customer empathy. Most teams treat those as opposite skills. One belongs to engineering, one belongs to support.

The best fraud leaders hold both at once. They can look at a transaction graph and see a story. They can read a customer complaint and see a signal. They know what it costs when you get it wrong, not just in dollars, but in customer trust and brand reputation.

I have spent 20 years in this discipline. The operators who win are the ones who refuse to let fraud become just a cost center. At Pinch we are building on exactly that idea.
